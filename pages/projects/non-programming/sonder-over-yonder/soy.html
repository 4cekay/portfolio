Sonder Over Yonder
← Back

Wonder alone? Wander together?

A social decision-making board game for 3-5 players where everyday scenarios are solved by using individual traits and enlisting the help of other unique characters, building connections that ultimately determine each player’s ending.

  • Course: Foundations of Game Design (SFU)
  • Development Period: March - April, 2026
  • Objective: Design and develop a transformative tabletop game, where the experience should evoke changes in thinking or feeling
  • Deliverables: Complete board game and physical prototype
  • Roles: System Designer, Narrative Designer & Writer, Presentation Leader & Video Editor
  • Tools: Figma, Game Design Documents, After Effects
  • Team Members: Jenny Nguyen, Victoria Lee, Kristyn Tsui, Tawshif Al Mehrab

Key Takeaways

  • Initial Ideation

    My team hit a standstill early on, stumped with the design objective–what theme could we effectively communicate through a physical game, where world building is limited and player engagement will rely mainly on mechanics and social atmosphere?

    Brainstorming started with a FigJam board where each of us could freely drop concepts, premises, or any other ideas we'd be interested in exploring, or creating a meaningful project out of. I grouped together similar ideas in hopes of finding overlaps that would strike inspiration for a viable gameplay system. The main concerns were:

    1. Themes that loomed over the narrative so heavily that they took the "Fun" part out of "Transformative Fun"
    2. Themes that were compelling, but needed more complex systems that could be served better through a digital environment

    One particular idea led to a conversation about interactions between strangers, the notion of protagonists and side characters in life, and the complex web of individual stories that make up our surroundings:

    Six Degrees of Separation: “A theory suggesting that any two people on Earth are connected by, at most, six social connections or intermediaries. Popularized through social psychology and network science, this concept highlights the surprising closeness of our global social network—and its implications for communication, influence, and behavior.”


    Koenig-Workman, I. (2025). Six Degrees of Separation. The Decision Lab

    This sparked the possibility of designing a Transformative Fun game centered around a lighter, more wholesome theme that still carries a meaningful message: Connection. One Google rabbit hole later led to the discovery of the term that eventually built the game’s premise:


    Sonder: “The realization and understanding that all other people have lives as complex as one's own.”


    Merriam-Webster Dictionary

    “You are the main character. The protagonist. The star at the center of your own unfolding story. You’re surrounded by your supporting cast: friends and family hanging in your immediate orbit..."

    “But there in the background, faint and out of focus, are the extras. The random passersby. Each living a life as vivid and complex as your own. They carry on invisibly around you, bearing the accumulated weight of their own ambitions, friends, routines, mistakes, worries, triumphs, and inherited craziness.

    "When your life moves on to the next scene, theirs flickers in place, wrapped in a cloud of backstory and inside jokes and characters strung together with countless other stories you’ll never be able to see. That you’ll never know exist. In which you might appear only once"


    John Koenig, author of "The Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows", who coined the term

  • Concept Finalists

    However, despite liking the idea of a connection, story-based, life simulation game, some of my team members were unsure of how to translate it into a tabletop game and leaned towards safer options that would be easier to accomplish for the course. Further ideation gave us the opportunity to elaborate practical mechanic concepts, at which point the project had narrowed down to two options:

    1. The Sonder life connections game,
    2. An environmental-preservation game focused on themes of climate change (either glaciers or coral reefs), where players take on the roles of nations negotiating policies to counteract the decline of natural habitats.

  • Solo Brainstorm Board

    I was already quite invested in the life-themed game and its narrative potential (and comedic, from the atmosphere-based fun angle), and my teammates had expressed finding it more interesting as well—they just weren't confident about the technical specifics.

    I didn’t want to give up on it yet, not without answering some of out development questions and determining that it really would be out of our scope for a course project. So I created a loose brainstorm web on my own time, allowing ideas from potential mechanics, theme connections, and reward systems to lay themselves out and try to formulate mechanic and dynamic links.

    I wound up with a big concept web to propose to my group, hoping to convince them that this was a challenge worth pursuing. It was messy and easy to get lost in many directions, but fortunately, the potential gameplay examples with connecting theme possibilities was enough to gain their votes of confidence. With some new inspirations, my teammates and I finally committed the idea.

  • Initial System & Theme Alignment

    After deciding on sets of unique elements for player character combinations (of careers and traits), the game’s early design was mapped out using the Mechanics→Dynamics→Aesthetics (MDA) Framework:

    1. physical components, rules, possible actions →
    2. resulting interactions between players →
    3. intended experiences

    The vision of a “transformative fun” game that was meaningful in all its ordinary became clearer after laying it all out; players each meandering through their own lives, with the option of choosing not to let others simply pass them by, with the invisible web of associations that grew with each interaction, was the underlying impact, not the encounters and stories themselves.
